Motion Along a Straight Line: Constant Acceleration
Constant Acceleration
If an object moves along a straight line and its acceleration is constant, we can say that the object has constant acceleration.
Integrate Relation of Velocity and Acceleration
Recalling that acceleration is the rate of change of velocity, shown as below, where a is acceleration, v is velocity, and t is time.
a = \frac{dv}{dt}       
We can easily rewrite the equation (1) as Integrate relation of velocity and acceleration.
v = \int adt       
As acceleration is constant, we can take it out of the integral sign.
v = a \int dt = at + c
When t= 0 ,
v_0 = a \cdot 0 + c = c
Finally, we get the equation of velocity as a function of time.
v(t) = v_0+ at

Consider a car that moves along a straight line with a constant acceleration of a = 8 \ \rm{m/s^2}. At t = 2 \ \rm{s} , we find that the velocity of the car is 13 \ \rm{m/s}. Find the initial velocity of the car at t = 0 \ \rm{s}.
Integrate Relation of Position and Velocity
Similarly, we can integrate the relation of position and velocity.
x = \int vdt
Putting (5) into (6), we get
x = \int (v_0+at)dt = v_0t + \frac{1}{2}at^2 + c
If we consider x = x_0 at t = 0, we can easily get
x_0 = 0 + 0 + c
and finally we get the equation of position as a function of time.
x(t) =x_0+ v_0t + \frac{1}{2}at^2
or
\Delta x = v_0t + \frac{1}{2}at^2

Free fall
Free fall is the process of an object falling from rest under the influence of gravity, where the acceleration due to gravity is constant and equals to g = 9.8 \ \rm{m/s^2}.
Example: Drop a Ball From a Height of {H} .
Assuming up is in the positive direction, since the acceleration due to gravity is -g, we can easily find the relation between height and time by using formula (9) ( v_0 = 0 because the object is dropped from rest).
y(t) = H - \frac{1}{2}gt^2
We can also find the velocity-height relation from (10).
\rm{(5)}\rm{(10)} \Rightarrow \left\{ \begin{array}{rcl} v(t) = -gt \\ t= \sqrt{2(H - y)/g} \end{array}\right. \Rightarrow v = - \sqrt{2g(H - y)}

Sample Problem #3
(a) Using the equations of motion:

        \begin{cases} v(t) = v_0 - g t\\y(t) = v_0 t - \frac{1}{2} g t^2 \end{cases}\Rightarrow \begin{cases} v(2) = 9.8\\y(2) = 39.2 \end{cases}

(b) The maximum height is reached when the velocity becomes zero:

        v(t_{\text{max}})= v_0-gt_{\text{max}} = 0\Rightarrow t_{\text{max}}= \frac{v_0}{g} = \frac{29.4}{9.8} = 3 \ \text{s}

        y_{\text{max}} = v_0 t_{\text{max}} - \frac{1}{2} g {t_{\text{max}}}^2 = 29.4 \times 3 - \frac{1}{2} \times 9.8 \times 3^2 = 44.1 \ \text{m}

(c) The ball hits the ground when y(t) = 0 . Using the equation:

        y(t) = v_0 t - \frac{1}{2} g t^2 = 29.4 t - \frac{1}{2} \times 9.8 t^2 = 0 \Rightarrow t = 0 \vee 6

Therefore, the ball hits the ground at t = 6 \ \text{s} .

Uniform Circular Motion
Introduction to Uniform Circular Motion
Uniform circular motion is a special type of two-dimensional motion in which an object moves in a fixed circular path at a constant speed. In this motion, although the magnitude of the speed is constant, its direction is constantly changing, so the object has a centripetal acceleration, always pointing towards the center of the circle.
Differential Relationships
In uniform circular motion, we can express the position of the object as:
r(t) = r \cos(\omega t) \hat{i} + r \sin(\omega t) \hat{j}
Where r is the radius of the circular motion, \omega is the angular velocity, representing the speed of the object's rotation. When the variation of \omega t is 2\pi, the object moves one circle along the circular motion, so we can also express the period as T = \frac{2\pi}{\omega}.
The velocity is the derivative of the position with respect to time, so we can get:
v(t) = \frac{dr(t)}{dt} = -r \omega \sin(\omega t) \hat{i} + r \omega \cos(\omega t) \hat{j}
The acceleration is the derivative of the velocity with respect to time, so we can get:
a(t) = \frac{dv(t)}{dt} = -r \omega^2 \cos(\omega t) \hat{i} - r \omega^2 \sin(\omega t) \hat{j}
From equation (3) we can see that the direction of acceleration is completely opposite to the direction of position, it is a vector pointing to the center, we can also call it centripetal acceleration.
Sample Problem #1
Three small balls are each suspended from three ropes performing uniform circular motion, and the radii are respectively r , 2r , 3r. If the angular velocities of the three small balls are 3\omega , 2\omega , \omega respectively, write out the position, velocity, and acceleration of the three small balls as a function of time.
Quantities of Each Physical Quantity
In uniform circular motion, the magnitude of the velocity can be easily obtained from equation (2) as shown in the following formula:
v = r \omega
We can also use a similar concept to get the magnitude of acceleration, as shown in the following formula:
a = r \omega^2
We can also get the relationship between the magnitude of velocity and acceleration from equations (5) and (6):
a = \frac{v^2}{r}
From equation (4) we can also express the period as
T = \frac{2\pi}{\omega} = \frac{2\pi r}{v}
From the above formula, we can also see that the physical meaning of the period can also be expressed as the distance of one circle divided by the magnitude of the velocity.

Sample Problem #1
Position Over Time
The position of the balls at any time t can be represented by the following equations:
\vec{r}_1(t) = r \cos(3\omega t) \hat{i} + r \sin(3\omega t) \hat{j}\\ \vec{r}_2(t) = 2r \cos(2\omega t) \hat{i} + 2r \sin(2\omega t) \hat{j}\\ \vec{r}_3(t) = 3r \cos(\omega t) \hat{i} + 3r \sin(\omega t) \hat{j}
Velocity Over Time
The velocity of the balls at any time t can be represented by the following equations:
\vec{v}_1(t) = -r 3\omega \sin(3\omega t) \hat{i} + r 3\omega \cos(3\omega t) \hat{j}\\ \vec{v}_2(t) = -2r 2\omega \sin(2\omega t) \hat{i} + 2r 2\omega \cos(2\omega t) \hat{j}\\ \vec{v}_3(t) = -3r \omega \sin(\omega t) \hat{i} + 3r \omega \cos(\omega t) \hat{j}
Acceleration Over Time
The acceleration of the balls at any time t can be represented by the following equations:
\vec{a}_1(t) = -r (3\omega)^2 \cos(3\omega t) \hat{i} - r (3\omega)^2 \sin(3\omega t) \hat{j}\\ \vec{a}_2(t) = -2r (2\omega)^2 \cos(2\omega t) \hat{i} - 2r (2\omega)^2 \sin(2\omega t) \hat{j}\\ \vec{a}_3(t) = -3r \omega^2 \cos(\omega t) \hat{i} - 3r \omega^2 \sin(\omega t) \hat{j}
Sample Problem #2
(a) Angular Velocity
The angular velocity \omega of the car can be calculated using the formula:
\omega = \frac{v}{r}
where v = 20 \, \text{m/s} is the speed of the car and r = 500 \, \text{m} is the radius of the circular path. Substituting these values gives \omega = \frac{20}{500} = 0.04 \, \text{s}^{-1}.
(b) Centripetal Acceleration
The centripetal acceleration a_c of the car can be calculated using the formula:
a_c = \omega^2 r
where \omega = 0.04 \, \text{s}^{-1} is the angular velocity and r = 500 \, \text{m} is the radius of the circular path. Substituting these values gives a_c = (0.04)^2 \times 500 = 0.8 \, \text{m/s}^2.
(c) Time to Complete One Full Circle
The time T it takes for the car to complete one full circle can be calculated using the formula:
T = \frac{2\pi r}{v}
where v = 20 \, \text{m/s} is the speed of the car and r = 500 \, \text{m} is the radius of the circular path. Substituting these values gives T = \frac{2\pi \times 500}{20} = 157.08 \, \text{s}.
